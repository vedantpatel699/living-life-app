# Living Life — Supplements

Evidence-based supplements for **your specific situation**: Indian lacto-vegetarian diet (milk yes, no eggs/meat/fish), living in **Canada**, ~78 kg, BMI ~28, starting back-to-fit journey. Already taking whey + creatine.

**Bottom line up top:** the only supplements with strong RCT evidence for your specific context are **creatine, whey protein, vitamin D3, vitamin B12, and algal omega-3**. Everything else is optional, conditional on blood tests, or marketing fluff.

> ⚠️ Two reality checks before we start. (1) I'm not a doctor — get the blood tests in §9 before starting anything new. (2) Supplement quality matters. Health Canada's Natural Health Products (NHP) framework is stricter than the US FDA — look for the **NPN number** (Natural Product Number) on the label, which means Health Canada has pre-market reviewed it for safety, quality, and label accuracy ([Health Canada NHP overview](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/drugs-health-products/natural-non-prescription/regulation.html)). Products with an NPN are meaningfully safer than the US "FDA-exempt" supplement market or unregulated Indian imports.

## How I'm grading evidence

Two frameworks I'm using throughout:

**Australian Institute of Sport (AIS) ABCD framework** ([AIS Supplements Framework](https://www.ausport.gov.au/ais/nutrition/supplements)) — the most rigorous publicly-available system:
- **Group A** — strong RCT evidence for specific situations (creatine, caffeine, beta-alanine, protein, electrolytes)
- **Group B** — emerging evidence, possibly useful (curcumin, tart cherry, collagen)
- **Group C** — insufficient evidence — *where most marketed supplements live*
- **Group D** — banned or risky

**Examine.com** grades supplement-outcome pairs A through F. E.g., creatine is A for strength/lean mass, C for fat loss.

If a supplement isn't Group A for your goal, the marketing is almost certainly bigger than the effect.

## 1. Creatine — confirmed staple (Group A)

You're already on it. Quick confirmation it's worth continuing and a small upgrade to the protocol if useful.

**Why it works for you specifically:** vegetarians have ~20-30% lower baseline muscle creatine stores than omnivores. Multiple RCTs show **vegetarians gain MORE muscle and strength from creatine supplementation than omnivores do** on the same protocol — your starting deficit is bigger, so the supplement closes more gap ([Burke et al. — Effect of creatine and weight training in vegetarians](https://www.paulogentil.com/pdf/Effect%20of%20Creatine%20and%20Weight%20Training%20on%20Muscle%20Creatine%20and%20Performance%20in%20Vegetarians.pdf); [Kaviani et al. 2020 systematic review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7246861/); [2024 confirmation study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12431585/)).

**Dose:** **5 g/day, daily, forever.** Skip the loading phase — it's optional and gives the same result by 3-4 weeks. Loading just causes GI upset for some people.

**Form:** **Creatine monohydrate.** Not HCl, not "buffered," not "magnesium chelate." Those are more expensive and no better. Look for **Creapure** on the label (German-manufactured, third-party tested).

**Timing:** doesn't matter much. Take it whenever you'll remember (morning coffee, breakfast, post-workout, before bed — all fine).

**Safety:** ~7,000 studies. Safe up to 30 g/day for years ([ISSN position stand on creatine](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5469049/)). One note: it slightly raises serum creatinine on lab tests (since creatine breaks down into creatinine). Tell any doctor doing a kidney panel that you supplement — this is a known false-flag.

**Brands in Canada:** Optimum Nutrition Micronized Creatine (Costco, Amazon.ca, Popeye's), AllMax Creatine, Now Foods Creatine Monohydrate (Well.ca, Amazon.ca), Bulk Barn Creapure-source. Costco's Kirkland Signature line doesn't carry creatine reliably — stick with ON or AllMax.

## 2. Whey protein — convenience tool, not magic (Group A)

You're on it. The honest framing: whey is a **food-replacement convenience tool**, not a special muscle-building substance.

**What matters is total daily protein.** The ISSN position stand (Jäger et al. 2017) recommends **1.4-2.0 g/kg/day** for exercising individuals — for you at 78 kg, that's **110-160 g/day**, with **~130-140 g** being a practical target during a fat-loss phase ([Jäger et al., JISSN 2017](https://jissn.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12970-017-0177-8)).

**Whey's job:** if hitting 130 g/day from food alone is hard, a scoop or two of whey (25-50 g) closes the gap fast. Each 25 g scoop also clears the per-meal leucine threshold (~2.5-3 g) that triggers muscle protein synthesis.

**Indian lacto-veg sources you should also count:**
- Paneer: ~18 g protein per 100 g
- Milk: ~3.3 g per 100 ml (a glass ≈ 8 g)
- Greek yogurt: ~9 g per 100 g (regular dahi: ~3-4 g/100 g)
- Cooked dal/moong/rajma: ~9 g per 100 g
- Tofu: ~8 g per 100 g
- Peanuts: ~26 g per 100 g
- Almonds: ~21 g per 100 g

A typical Indian veg day: 2 glasses milk (16 g) + 100 g paneer (18 g) + dal twice (~18 g) + curd (6 g) + nuts (10 g) + 1 whey scoop (25 g) = **~95 g.** You'll need to slightly oversample protein at each meal to hit 130 g — that's why a single daily whey scoop is the cleanest fix.

**Form:** Whey **concentrate** (WPC, 70-80% protein) is fine and cheapest. **Isolate** (WPI, 90%+) only if you're lactose-sensitive.

**Brands available in Canada worth trusting:**
- **Costco Canada:** Optimum Nutrition Gold Standard (5lb tub ~$80 CAD), occasionally Dymatize ISO100. Best price-per-gram in Canada.
- **Amazon.ca / Well.ca:** Dymatize ISO100, Allmax IsoFlex, MyProtein Impact (Informed Sport batches), Naked Whey, Now Foods Whey
- **Popeye's Supplements / GNC Canada:** ON Gold Standard, AllMax, BSN, larger selection but premium pricing
- **Bulk Barn:** unflavored whey concentrate at the lowest $/g if you want to flavor yourself

**Look for the NPN number** on the label — this confirms Health Canada has reviewed the product. Brands without an NPN are still legal in Canada but less rigorously vetted.

## 3. Vitamin D3 — you almost certainly need this (Group A for deficient)

**Why this isn't optional for you in Canada:** Canadian winters mean ~5 months a year (October through April depending on latitude) where the sun angle is too low for skin to synthesize meaningful Vitamin D from UVB exposure — no matter how much you go outside. Statistics Canada's CCHS data shows **roughly 32-40% of Canadians have insufficient serum 25(OH)D (<50 nmol/L)**, with rates running much higher in **South Asian, Black, and East Asian Canadians** (darker skin produces less vitamin D per unit of UVB), and in **winter months specifically** ([Statistics Canada CCHS data](https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/82-625-x/2013001/article/11727-eng.htm); [PMC review on Canadian vitamin D status](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7232036/)).

You hit multiple risk multipliers: South Asian skin pigmentation, indoor lifestyle, Canadian latitude, and starting from sedentary. **Even without testing, low-dose D3 (1,000-2,000 IU/day) is very low risk and probably justified.** With testing, you can dose precisely.

Health Canada's official RDA is 600 IU/day, but most clinical evidence supports 1,000-2,000 IU/day for adults — and **Osteoporosis Canada recommends 800-2,000 IU/day** for adults under 50 and 800-2,000+ IU/day for adults over 50 ([Osteoporosis Canada Vitamin D recommendations](https://osteoporosis.ca/calcium-vitamin-d/)).

**Form: D3 (cholecalciferol) only.** D3 raises blood levels ~87% more efficiently than D2 (ergocalciferol) ([Tripkovic et al. meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3349454/)).

**Target serum 25(OH)D:** 30-50 ng/ml. Above 80 unclear benefit. Above 100 approaching toxicity.

**Dose ladder if you test** (Canadian labs report in nmol/L — multiply ng/ml × 2.5 = nmol/L):
| Your 25(OH)D | What to do |
|---|---|
| <30 nmol/L (severe deficiency, <12 ng/ml) | 50,000 IU once a week for 8-12 weeks (prescription dose, ask GP), then maintenance |
| 30-50 nmol/L (deficient, 12-20 ng/ml) | 2,000-4,000 IU/day for 12 weeks, retest |
| 50-75 nmol/L (insufficient, 20-30 ng/ml) | 1,000-2,000 IU/day |
| >75 nmol/L (sufficient, >30 ng/ml) | 800-1,000 IU/day maintenance, especially Oct-April |

**Take with breakfast** that contains fat (paneer, paratha with ghee, nuts) — D3 is fat-soluble.

**K2 question:** combination D3+K2 products are popular. Evidence that K2 specifically reduces cardiovascular calcification in healthy people is limited. Not essential at normal D3 doses; not harmful if you want to take it.

**Brands in Canada:**
- **Costco Kirkland Signature Vitamin D3 1,000 IU** — 400 softgels for ~$13 CAD. Absurd value, NPN-licensed, lasts >1 year.
- **Webber Naturals D3 1,000 IU or 2,500 IU** — Shoppers Drug Mart, Walmart, Amazon.ca. NPN-licensed, frequently on sale.
- **Jamieson Vitamin D3** — wide availability, NPN-licensed.
- **Now Foods D3** — Well.ca, Amazon.ca. Imported, NPN-licensed.

If your GP confirms severe deficiency, they can prescribe **D-Drops** (1,000 IU per drop, easy dosing) or **50,000 IU weekly capsules** for the loading protocol.

## 4. Vitamin B12 — almost certainly needed (Group A for vegetarians)

**Why this isn't optional for you:** B12 is essentially absent from plant foods. Western/Canadian lacto-vegetarians still show elevated B12 deficiency rates — meta-analyses report **62% of vegetarians worldwide have low B12 status** ([Pawlak et al. — How prevalent is vitamin B12 deficiency among vegetarians?](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23356638/)), and Canadian studies on plant-forward eaters confirm the pattern ([CMAJ vitamin B12 article](https://www.cmaj.ca/content/189/24/E835); [Vegetarian Nutrition Group position paper](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27886704/)).

Dairy alone gives only ~1 µg per cup of milk; typical lacto-veg dairy intake gets you to ~1-2 µg/day, vs the Health Canada RDA of 2.4 µg/day. Lacto-veg ≠ B12-replete.

**Form: methylcobalamin or cyanocobalamin** — both work. Methylcobalamin is more available in India and is the active form. Cyanocobalamin is slightly better absorbed in the gut but converts to methyl in your body. Practical advice: **whichever is easier to buy.** Differences are clinically minor.

**Dose, pick one:**
- **Daily**: 250-500 µg sublingual methylcobalamin
- **Weekly**: 2,500-5,000 µg sublingual once per week (higher doses absorb ~1-2% via passive diffusion regardless of stomach acid)

**Testing:** serum B12 alone is unreliable. The functional markers are **homocysteine** (elevated if B12 deficient) and **methylmalonic acid (MMA)** (more specific to B12, harder to find in India). If you can, ask for serum B12 + homocysteine.

**Brands in Canada:**
- **Webber Naturals B12 Methylcobalamin 1,000 mcg** — Shoppers, Costco, Amazon.ca. NPN-licensed, ~$10-15 CAD for 90 sublingual tablets.
- **Jamieson B12** — wide availability.
- **Now Foods Methyl B12 1,000 mcg or 5,000 mcg** — Well.ca, Amazon.ca. The 5,000 mcg is good if you go weekly.
- **AOR B12 Cobalamin** — premium brand, NPN-licensed.

## 5. Omega-3 (EPA/DHA from algal oil) — vegetarian-specific (Group A)

**Why this isn't optional for you:** humans convert plant ALA (flax, chia, walnuts) to EPA at only **5-10%**, and to DHA at **<1%** ([Davis & Kris-Etherton review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523048955); [bioavailability scoping review 2021](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10408398.2021.1880364)). Eating walnuts daily does not reliably raise blood EPA/DHA. Vegetarians measurably have lower omega-3 index than fish-eaters.

The solution: **algal-oil EPA+DHA.** Algae are the original source — fish accumulate it by eating algae. Algal DHA has bioavailability equivalent to DHA from cooked salmon ([NIH ODS Omega-3 factsheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Omega3FattyAcids-HealthProfessional/)).

**Dose:** **500-1,000 mg combined EPA+DHA/day.** Higher (1.5-3 g/day) if you have elevated triglycerides — South Asians tend to have high TG and low HDL, so a lipid panel will tell you.

**Read the label carefully:** many "omega-3" products quote *total omega-3* including ALA, which doesn't count for you. You want **EPA + DHA explicitly listed**.

**Take with breakfast** — fat-soluble, absorbs better with food fat.

**Brands in Canada (must be ALGAL not fish):**
- **Nordic Naturals Algae Omega** — Well.ca, Amazon.ca, premium brand, NPN-licensed, ~$45 CAD/60-day supply
- **Ovega-3** — Well.ca, Walmart Canada
- **Garden of Life Mykind Vegan Omega-3** — Whole Foods, Amazon.ca, NPN-licensed
- **Sports Research Algae Omega-3** — Amazon.ca import
- **Webber Naturals Vegan Algal-3** — Costco/Shoppers, more affordable Canadian option, NPN-licensed

Avoid generic "vegan omega-3" labels that quote only total omega-3 (often mostly ALA flaxseed oil padding) — verify EPA + DHA totals explicitly.

## 6. Conditional — test first, supplement only if indicated

### Iron — DO NOT supplement blind

You're male, you don't menstruate, and absorption ramps down when stores are full. Chronic over-supplementation can cause iron overload — *not harmless*.

**Test first**: ferritin (iron stores). Target for men: 30-200 ng/ml.
- **<30 ng/ml** → depleted; treat with food first (palak, methi, soaked rajma, dates, beetroot) + vitamin C at the same meal (lemon, amla). If still low after 8 weeks, supplement ferrous bisglycinate 30-65 mg elemental iron — separate from dairy/calcium/tea/coffee by 2 hours.
- **30-200 ng/ml** → fine, don't supplement.
- **>200 ng/ml + elevated transferrin sat** → see a doctor.

### Zinc — probably fine if you eat varied

Indian veg diet phytates inhibit zinc absorption. If you eat traditional preparations (idli/dosa fermented, sprouted dals, well-soaked legumes) zinc bioavailability is meaningfully better.

**Dose if supplementing:** 15-25 mg/day, with food (avoid nausea). Long-term high-dose zinc depletes copper — don't run >25 mg/day for >3 months without occasional copper (cashews, dark chocolate, sesame).

**Cleanest path:** if you're already taking a men's multivitamin, you have zinc covered.

### Calcium — almost certainly NOT needed

Your dairy intake likely covers 800-1,200 mg/day calcium already. **Supplemental calcium has been linked to modestly increased CV risk in some meta-analyses; food-source calcium is not** ([Yang et al. 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/2/368); [meta-analysis 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10111600/); [MESA study](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/jaha.116.003815)). Skip supplements; eat your paneer.

### Iodine — usually OK in Canada

Canadian table salt has been iodized since 1949 and most processed foods using table salt deliver enough iodine ([Health Canada iodine factsheet](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/food-nutrition/healthy-eating/vitamins-minerals/dietary-reference-intakes-elements.html)). If you mostly use kosher salt, sea salt, or Himalayan pink salt (which are NOT iodized), or if you eat very few processed foods, you could be low. **Test TSH instead of supplementing iodine blind** — TSH out of range is the meaningful signal.

### Magnesium — only if sleep is a problem

Evidence is Tier B-C for sleep/recovery. A 2024 systematic review found "modest positive results" ([PMC11136869](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11136869/)).

**If you try it:** **magnesium glycinate** (not oxide — oxide is a laxative with poor absorption). 200-400 mg 30-60 min before bed. If your sleep is fine, skip.

## 7. Performance optionals (if you want them)

### Caffeine (Group A)

**Strong evidence** for endurance, sprints, and resistance training volume at **3-6 mg/kg ~45 min pre-exercise** ([ISSN caffeine position stand 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7777221/); [2025 network meta-analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3792)).

For you (78 kg): **200-300 mg** ≈ 2 strong coffees or a caffeine pill ~45 min before your workout.

**Hard rule: no caffeine after 2 PM** if you train mornings or care about sleep. Caffeine half-life is 5-6h; for slow metabolizers, 8-10h.

Skip on rest days if you can to preserve sensitivity.

### Beta-alanine (Group A — situational)

**Only relevant for 1-4 minute high-intensity efforts** (400-800m run intervals, hard cycle intervals) ([Hobson 2012 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22270875/)). **No acute pre-workout effect** — this is a chronic loading supplement (3-4 weeks of daily dosing before benefit).

**Verdict for you right now: not yet.** You're not running intervals. Reconsider when you're chasing performance in Phase 3.

### Ashwagandha — Indian context, real but modest evidence

Indian Ayurvedic tradition, **but separate from RCT evidence**. The actual RCTs at 300-600 mg/day KSM-66 extract for 8+ weeks show:
- Modest improvement in **stress and sleep quality** ([Cheah et al. 2021 meta-analysis](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0257843); [NIH ODS Ashwagandha factsheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Ashwagandha-HealthProfessional/))
- Some strength/testosterone trials show benefits, BUT many are industry-funded; interpret cautiously

**Cautions:**
- Affects thyroid (raises T4, suppresses TSH activity) — **get TSH tested before starting** if you have any thyroid history
- Rare hepatotoxicity case reports
- Don't run continuously >3-6 months

**If you try it:** 300-600 mg/day KSM-66 standardized root extract, evening, for 8-12 weeks.

### Citrulline malate — meh

Modest evidence at 6-8 g pre-workout for high-rep volume and pump. ([Meta-analysis 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34010809/)). **Skip unless you specifically want the pump feeling.** Marginal effect.

### Multivitamin (iron-free, men's)

Mixed evidence in healthy adults. The "insurance" argument has merit for vegetarians filling micronutrient gaps without taking 5 separate pills.

**If you'd rather take 1 pill than 4:** a once-daily iron-free men's multi covers B12, B6, folate, zinc, iodine, selenium, modest D3, antioxidants. Examples: Centrum Men, Nature's Bounty Men's, Carbamide Forte Daily Multivitamin (Indian), Now Foods ADAM.

**Don't double-up:** if you take a multi, you don't separately need B12 and zinc tablets (verify doses match what's in the multi though).

## 7.5. Shift-work supplements (Tier 4 — for your Amazon-shift nights)

Working night shifts is its own physiological challenge. Sleep quality drops, cortisol rhythm flips, and the standard supplement timing breaks. This section is research-curated specifically for the 1:20-11:30 AM shift cycle with daytime sleep at 12:30 PM – 8:30 PM. **None of these are essential** — start with sleep hygiene + the Tier 1 core stack — but each has decent evidence for shift workers specifically.

The schedule in `data/supplements.json` adds a **pre-sleep slot** (~12:00 PM on shift days) where these would slot in.

### 7.5a. Melatonin — low-dose, only on shift days

- **Dose:** 0.3–0.5 mg sublingual or fast-dissolve tablet. **NOT** 3–5 mg — those supraphysiological doses cause morning grogginess and may blunt your own production.
- **Why it works for shift workers specifically:** at low doses melatonin mimics physiological levels and acts as a circadian phase-shifter + mild hypnotic. Your body's own melatonin doesn't release at 12:30 PM — you're trying to override daylight to sleep.
- **Evidence quality:** **Strong** (the strongest in this section). Cochrane systematic review of melatonin for shift workers ([Liira 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25113164/)) found melatonin increases daytime sleep duration by ~24 min on average. No clear improvement in shift-time alertness, but the sleep benefit is the goal.
- **Timing:** 30 min before sleep — so ~12:00 PM on shift days. Skip on off days unless adjusting back to night sleep.
- **Vegetarian/Canada flag:** lacto-veg friendly. Get veg-cap brands (Jamieson, Natural Factors, Webber). Health Canada NPN required. OTC.
- **Cost:** ~$8–12 CAD/month.

### 7.5b. Magnesium glycinate (bisglycinate) — daily, especially shift days

- **Dose:** 200–400 mg elemental magnesium, as bisglycinate (NOT oxide or citrate — those cause GI issues).
- **Why:** GABA-A modulation + NMDA antagonism support sleep onset. Shift workers tend to have lower Mg status from disrupted intake. Also pairs naturally with creatine + electrolytes if you're losing it through sweat.
- **Evidence:** **Moderate.** [Mah & Pitre 2021 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34883514/) — ~17 min faster sleep onset in adults with insomnia symptoms. Effect is real, but modest.
- **Timing:** 30–60 min before sleep — pre-sleep slot on shift days, normal bedtime off-shift.
- **Vegetarian/Canada flag:** fully lacto-veg. CanPrev, Natural Factors, Pure Encapsulations widely stocked.
- **Cost:** ~$15–25 CAD/month.

### 7.5c. L-theanine — flexible (shift + pre-sleep)

- **Dose:** 100–200 mg. Suntheanine-branded versions are the studied form.
- **Why:** alpha-wave promotion → calm focus without sedation. Two uses for night shifts: **(a)** pre-sleep to dampen residual caffeine arousal at 12:00 PM, **(b)** mid-shift paired 1:1 or 2:1 with caffeine for smoother alertness with less crash.
- **Evidence:** **Moderate.** [Williams 2020 systematic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31758301/) supports anxiolytic + attention benefits, especially in the caffeine-pairing use.
- **Timing:** pre-sleep dose 30 min before 12:30 PM sleep; shift dose pairs with your shift caffeine (before 5:30 AM cutoff).
- **Vegetarian/Canada flag:** lacto-veg OK. Widely available (Now, Jamieson).
- **Cost:** ~$10–18 CAD/month.

### 7.5d. Ashwagandha — **NOT for you** (SSRI interaction)

You're on fluoxetine (SSRI). Ashwagandha has mild serotonergic and GABAergic activity, plus documented case reports of cholestatic liver injury — none of which you want stacked on an SSRI you're already metabolizing through the liver. Fluoxetine's very long half-life (parent ~4-6 days, active metabolite norfluoxetine ~7-15 days) also means any combination effect persists long after stopping. **Skip ashwagandha entirely** for as long as you're on fluoxetine. If you ever change meds, revisit with your GP first.

If you want a cortisol-regulation strategy that's SSRI-safe, the highest-leverage interventions are non-supplement: cooler/darker sleep room on shift days, magnesium glycinate (below), L-theanine (below), and protecting the 12:30 PM – 8:30 PM sleep window aggressively. Those move the needle more than any adaptogen.

### 7.5e. Glycine — optional, cheap, pre-sleep

- **Dose:** 3 g powder or capsules.
- **Why:** lowers core body temperature via peripheral vasodilation — mimics natural sleep-onset thermoregulation. Useful when you're sleeping at 12:30 PM in a room that's getting warmer through the afternoon.
- **Evidence:** **Weak-to-moderate.** Small Japanese RCTs ([Yamadera 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22529837/), [Bannai 2012]) show subjective sleep improvement; no large independent replication. Safe and cheap, so the bar to try is low.
- **Timing:** 30–60 min before sleep — mix powder in water or take capsules.
- **Vegetarian/Canada flag:** synthetic glycine is lacto-veg. Bulk powder is cheapest.
- **Cost:** ~$5–10 CAD/month.

### Skipped / deprioritized for shift workers

| Supplement | Why I'm not recommending it for you |
|---|---|
| **Apigenin / chamomile** | Marketed heavily (Huberman effect); human data is thin. One small postpartum-women RCT; no shift-work data. Mechanism plausible, evidence preliminary. |
| **Tart cherry / Montmorency** | Trace melatonin (~0.1–0.3 mg/serving) + anthocyanins. To get a meaningful dose you'd pay $40–60 CAD/month — direct melatonin is cheaper and dose-controlled. Anti-inflammatory angle is redundant with your algal omega-3. |
| **Rhodiola rosea** | Modest evidence for mental fatigue ([Olsson 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19437330/)). Effect is small and redundant with your caffeine strategy. Could trial as non-caffeine alertness option post-cutoff. Low priority. |
| **Probiotics** | Shift work disrupts gut microbiome ([Voigt 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25272479/)), but trials of specific strains are very mixed. Food sources (dahi, kefir) are a better first step. |
| **Vitamin K2 MK-7** | Synergy with D3 is theoretically appealing but no shift-work evidence. Optional, not shift-relevant. |
| **CoQ10 / ubiquinol** | No good evidence for fatigue/energy in healthy adults. Reserved for statin users / specific mitochondrial conditions. Skip. |

### Shift supplement cost summary (your SSRI-safe version)

- **Conservative shift add-on (melatonin + Mg-glycinate):** ~$23–37 CAD/month.
- **Full SSRI-safe shift stack (melatonin + Mg-glycinate + L-theanine + glycine):** ~$38–65 CAD/month.
- Ashwagandha removed — see §7.5d.

### How to start

Don't add all four at once — you won't know what's helping. Order:
1. **Week 1–2:** add **low-dose melatonin (0.3-0.5 mg)** on shift days only. Track sleep duration (Health Connect sleep stages) and how you feel waking up at 8:30 PM.
2. **Week 3–4:** add **magnesium glycinate** daily. Same tracking.
3. **Week 5+:** if still struggling post-shift wind-down, add **L-theanine** pre-sleep on shift days.
4. **Glycine** is a cheap optional add-on — come in anytime.
5. **Flag any of these to your prescribing GP** at your next medication review, so they're aware of your full stack.

| **BCAAs** | Adequate total protein makes BCAAs redundant. They alone can't elevate muscle protein synthesis without other essential AAs ([Wolfe 2017, JISSN](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5568273/)). |
| **Glutamine** | No meaningful effect in healthy lifters. Some evidence in burn/critical patients only. |
| **Fat burners / thermogenics** | Mostly caffeine + filler + side effects. Just take caffeine. |
| **Test boosters / DHEA / tribulus** | No significant effect on T or strength in men with normal levels ([tribulus review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378874105003314); [Kovac et al.](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1557988315598554)). Save money. |
| **Detox / cleanse pills** | No mechanism. Your liver and kidneys do this. |
| **CLA** | Inconsistent meta-analyses; effectively null ([2023 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37671495/)). |
| **Garcinia cambogia** | Null trials; case reports of liver injury. |
| **Apple cider vinegar pills** | Tiny effect on post-meal glycemia; nothing for weight loss; damages enamel. |
| **Pre-workout "proprietary blends"** | Hides doses. Buy caffeine + creatine + (optionally) citrulline individually — cheaper, you know what you're getting. |
| **Mass gainers** | It's just sugar + cheap protein. Eat more dal-rice-paneer. |

## 9. Blood tests to do BEFORE starting anything new

In Canada, **most of these are covered by provincial health insurance when ordered by your family doctor.** Book a physical with your GP and ask them to order a fitness/wellness baseline panel — they can tick most of these tests if you explain you're starting an exercise program. If your GP won't order certain tests (vitamin D and B12 are sometimes deemed "not medically necessary" by some provinces), you can pay out-of-pocket at LifeLabs / Dynacare / Alpha Labs — typically **$30-80 CAD per test** with a private requisition (LifeLabs MyVisit or similar).

| Test | What it tells you | Good range (adult male) — Canadian units |
|---|---|---|
| **25-hydroxyvitamin D** | D status | 75-125 nmol/L sufficient |
| **Vitamin B12 (serum)** | B12 status (coarse) | >220 pmol/L comfortable (Canadian labs) |
| **Homocysteine** | Functional B12/folate marker | <12 µmol/L |
| **Ferritin** | Iron stores | 30-300 µg/L for men |
| **Iron + TIBC + transferrin sat** | Iron handling | TSAT 20-50% |
| **CBC** | Anemia, basic hematology | Hb >130 g/L |
| **TSH (+ Free T4)** | Thyroid screen | TSH 0.4-4.0 mIU/L |
| **HbA1c + fasting glucose** | Insulin resistance baseline | HbA1c <5.7%, fasting <5.6 mmol/L |
| **Lipid panel** | CV risk baseline | LDL <2.6 mmol/L, TG <1.7 mmol/L, HDL >1.0 mmol/L |
| **ALT/AST** | Liver baseline | Standard reference |
| **Creatinine + eGFR** | Kidney baseline (creatine note: see §1) | Standard reference |

**South Asian-specific note (still applies to you in Canada):** South Asian genetic background means you develop diabetes and dyslipidemia at lower BMI than European reference. At BMI 28 sedentary baseline, glycemic + lipid screening is genuinely useful — not paranoia. The Canadian Cardiovascular Society explicitly recognizes South Asian ethnicity as a CV risk modifier ([CCS guidelines on dyslipidemia](https://ccs.ca/guidelines-and-position-statements/)).

**Cadence:** baseline now → recheck the supplemented markers at 3 months → full panel annually.

## 12. Quality checks — how to not get ripped off

**Buy from reputable channels:** Nutrabay, HealthKart, Amazon India ("sold and fulfilled by" reputable seller verified), 1mg, PharmEasy. Avoid Instagram drop-shippers and "70% off" deals from sources you don't know.

**Look for third-party certifications:**
- **NSF Certified for Sport** (gold standard — banned substances + content accuracy)
- **Informed Sport / Informed Choice** (batch-tested)
- **USP Verified** (content accuracy + GMP audit)

These certifications are rare on India-manufactured products and more common on imported US/EU brands.

**Red flags that mean don't buy:**
- "Proprietary blend" hiding individual doses
- Claims like "30% fat loss in 30 days" or "10x more effective"
- Brands you've never heard of with no third-party testing
- Pre-workouts with "amphetamine-like" or unfamiliar stimulant names (DMAA, DMHA, oxilofrine — these are AIS Group D banned)

## 13. Honest limits and uncertainty

- Creatine response in vegetarians is well-supported in direction but variable in magnitude — you'll respond, the question is by how much.
- The "70-90% vitamin D deficient" and "47-77% B12 deficient" figures are population summaries — your individual status depends on diet, sun exposure, genetics. Test if you can.
- Ashwagandha trials are disproportionately funded by extract manufacturers; strength/T results overstated, stress/sleep effects more reliable.
- Multivitamin evidence is genuinely mixed; some studies show small cancer/cognitive benefits, others null. It's a low-leverage decision either way.
- No supplement listed here will meaningfully shrink your lipomas (see PLAN.md and METHODOLOGY.md §8 — that's a medical conversation, not a nutrition one).
